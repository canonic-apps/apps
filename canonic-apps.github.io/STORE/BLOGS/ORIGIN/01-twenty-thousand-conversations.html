Twenty Thousand Conversations

What patients taught us about governance

Bloomberg Summit, 2017

New York City. I'm presenting "Breast We Can!" at the Bloomberg Data for Health Summit. Crowdsourced AI for breast cancer detection. The pitch is simple: patients are generating imaging data. That data could save lives. The system is broken.

The room is full of healthcare executives and policy makers. Nobody's built this yet. The technology is emerging. The regulations are unclear. The opportunity is massive.

UCF signs on. Western Digital provides storage infrastructure. NIH grants start flowing. $5M+ in funding over the next seven years. Clinical trials at UCSF.

We call it MammoChat.

Casey DeSantis

2021. Florida's First Lady is diagnosed with breast cancer. She goes public with her journey. The state launches awareness campaigns. The conversation about early detection reaches millions.

Meanwhile, MammoChat is quietly logging its ten-thousandth patient conversation. Women across the country, navigating the same fear, the same uncertainty, the same gaps in communication that the healthcare system leaves unfilled.

We're not competing with doctors. We're filling the silence between appointments.

The Pattern

Twenty thousand conversations later, a pattern emerges.

Patients don't need AI to replace their doctors. They need AI to fill the gaps. The 2 AM anxiety. The confusing paperwork. The silence that stretches between "we found something" and "here's what it means."

Every conversation generates evidence. Every evidence point needs protection. Every protection needs rules.

The rules accumulate. The rules need governance.

What We Learned

From twenty thousand conversations:

Patients remember how they felt more than what they learned.

The information matters. The tone matters more. The consistency matters most.

An AI that gives perfect information but varies wildly in approach breeds distrust. An AI that maintains steady presence, clear boundaries, and honest limitations earns something harder to measure: confidence.

The Question

How do you ensure that consistency at scale?

Not through training alone. Models change. Prompts drift. Teams rotate. The thing that seemed safe yesterday becomes problematic tomorrow.

You need something that persists. Something the AI can read. Something humans can audit. Something that doesn't depend on who's watching.

You need governance that governs itself.

From Patients to Protocol

MammoChat didn't set out to build a governance framework. It set out to help scared women navigate a healthcare system that often fails them.

But the conversations demanded it. Each edge case. Each moment where "what should I say?" became "what should anyone say in this situation, forever?"

The patients taught us. Every constraint in CANONIC traces back to a real person, a real conversation, a real moment where we learned something couldn't be left to chance.
